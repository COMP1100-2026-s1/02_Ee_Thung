## Learning Card 5: Intimidating Sign Up Process

**Refer to** : Test Card 5

**We believed that** students feel that the current club discovery and sign-up process is fragmented, frustrating, and intimidating, creating real barriers that prevent them from joining clubs

**We observed that** 18 out of 20 students interviewed reported at least one significant friction point in the current process. 75% cited information being scattered across Instagram, Discord, emails, and the UQ Union website as their primary frustration. Market Day overcrowding, the fear of not knowing anyone, and confusing sign-up emails were also consistently raised as intimidating barriers to participation.

**From this we learnt that** the barrier to club participation is not a lack of interest, but a systemlevel failure. Fragmented information, social anxiety, and no post-sign-up follow-through collectively prevent motivated students from ever making the leap to joining.

**Therefore, we will** ACCEPT this hypothesis and build our three-pillar solution to directly address each friction layer: centralised information (Unified Event Ecosystem), social safety (Skill-Matched Open Mabar), and personalised onboarding (1-on-1 Consultation Hub)

## Learning Card 6: UX Preferences for Club Discovery

**Refer to** : Test card 6

**We believed that** students have clear, converging preferences for how they browse and filter clubs, and identifying these will inform our MVP UX design.

**We observed that** approximately 75% of students preferred category or interest-based tab filtering as their primary discovery method, making it the clear dominant preference. Keyword search was the second most cited option. However, secondary preferences diverged significantly — students individually requested commitment-level filters, degree-based filtering, personality quizzes, price filters, and personalised recommendation feeds

**From this we learnt that** while category browsing is a clear MVP priority, students have meaningfully different secondary needs based on their personal constraints. A single filtering method will not serve all segments effectively.

**Therefore, we will**  ACCEPT this hypothesis. Category-based filtering should anchor the MVP UX. Keyword search should be included as a secondary layer, while commitment-level and price filtering are scoped for a future iteration.

## Learning Card 7: Unmet Feature Needs Beyond the ThreePillar Solution

**Refer to** : Test card 7

**We believed that** students have specific feature expectations and unmet needs that are not currently addressed by our three-pillar solution, revealing gaps in our MVP scope.

**We observed that** 18 out of 20 students (90%) suggested at least one feature not currently in our solution. The most recurring requests were real photos and candid media from past events, upcoming event calendars per club, club activity or recency indicators, membership cost transparency, and event reminder notifications. A community forum for peer recommendations was also raised.

**From this we learnt that** our three pillars address the core discovery and social barriers but leave significant gaps in ongoing engagement and contextual richness. Students want enough information to make a confident decision before committing — something the current UQ Union club pages fail to provide.

**Therefore, we will** ACCEPT this hypothesis. Event calendars and real media should be prioritised for the MVP club profile. Activity indicators, pricing transparency, and notification systems are scoped as high-priority additions for the next iteration.

## Learning Card 8: One-on-One Consultations Reduce Hesitation

**Refer to** : Test Card 8

**We believed that** one-on-one consultations with student ambassadors or club leaders would meaningfully reduce student hesitation and increase their confidence to join clubs, making it a critical feature for our most vulnerable segments.

**We observed that** 16 out of 20 students (80%) said the 1-on-1 booking feature would increase their confidence, meeting the 70% threshold. However, only approximately 10 out of 20 (50%) said they would realistically use it, falling just short of the 55% target. A consistent pattern emerged: students responded far more positively when the feature was framed as casual and optional rather than as a formal scheduled meeting.

**From this we learnt that** the 1-on-1 feature holds genuine value, but real-world uptake depends heavily on how it is positioned. Students want a low-stakes human bridge into club life — not a gatekeeping step. Those who are already socially confident saw little need for it at all.

**Therefore, we will** ACCEPT this hypothesis. The feature should remain in the solution but be redesigned to feel casual and optional (e.g., reframed as 'Quick Chat' or 'Ask a Member'). It must be presented as a resource, not a required onboarding step.
